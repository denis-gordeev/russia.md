Russian culture is often introduced through canonical writers, but literature matters not only because of prestige. It has long functioned as a public language for ethics, power, religion, violence, modernization, and the meaning of the state.

The Russian language became a major integrative force across the empire and the Soviet Union, yet it always coexisted with many other languages and local identities. That tension between a shared literary language and a multinational reality remains central.

Memory is equally important. War remembrance, Soviet experience, imperial inheritance, Orthodoxy, and regional identity all shape how cultural life is narrated. To understand Russian culture, it is necessary to read both the canon and the institutions that decide which past is emphasized.
