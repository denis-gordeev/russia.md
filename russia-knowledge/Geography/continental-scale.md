Russia is not just large. It is geographically discontinuous in the lived sense: European capitals, industrial river basins, Arctic coastlines, mountain frontiers, deep taiga, steppe agriculture, and Pacific ports all sit inside one state.

Distance changes almost every policy question. Transport costs, military planning, regional inequality, climate adaptation, and digital connectivity are all harder at this scale. Eleven time zones are not a curiosity; they are an administrative fact that shapes work, media, logistics, and governance.

The country’s river systems, forests, permafrost zones, and access points to Europe, the Arctic, the Caucasus, Central Asia, and East Asia make geography inseparable from politics. Russia’s geography is one reason the state has historically prioritized central control, rail and energy infrastructure, and strategic depth.
